Atomic Orbitals, Music, and the Early Universe

If you've seen pictures of atomic orbitals in chemistry, the left panel should look familiar. It shows a single spherical harmonic as a three-dimensional wave pattern. The numbers ℓ and m determine the pattern's complexity and orientation—you may have used them to understand how electrons fill atomic orbitals. Move the sliders and see what happens.

The right panel shows a simulated Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) sky. It is formed by superposing many spherical harmonics with different amplitudes. Increasing the maximum value of ℓ adds progressively finer wave patterns, revealing more detailed structure in the simulated universe.

Checking Show only current (ℓ, m) displays the contribution of one selected spherical harmonic—the analogue of a sine or cosine wave on the surface of a sphere. Unchecking it returns you to the full superposition of many spherical harmonics.

Pressing Build Universe starts an animation that adds modes up to the current maximum ℓ value on the slider. Notice how the largest features appear first, while smaller details emerge as higher-order harmonics are added. This is the same mathematical framework cosmologists use to describe the temperature fluctuations of the real CMB.

Maybe you've heard someone online break down a song into “stems.” Software can separate the bass, guitar, keyboards, and vocals. These programs use Fourier methods to decompose complex sounds into simpler components that can later be recombined to reproduce the song.

The Cosmic Microwave Background is the sky-song of the early universe. Just as a musical chord can be decomposed into individual notes, the temperature pattern of the CMB can be decomposed into spherical harmonics. The real CMB contains significant power well beyond ℓ = 200. Reproducing those finer details requires the superposition of tens of thousands of spherical harmonic modes—far more computation than is practical in a web browser.
